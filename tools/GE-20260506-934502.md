### The technique
Liquid's `sort` filter is stable — when two items are equal on the sort key, they preserve their relative order from the previous sort. This enables multi-key sorting with a two-pass approach: sort by the secondary key first, then sort by the primary key. Equal-primary items retain their secondary ordering.

```liquid
{% assign by_order = site.articles | sort: 'order' %}
{% assign sorted = by_order | sort: 'date' | reverse %}
```

Result: articles sorted by date descending, with same-date articles ordered by their `order` field ascending.

### Context
Jekyll articles in a series all share the same publication date. A plain `sort: 'date' | reverse` produces unpredictable ordering within that date. Adding an `order: N` frontmatter field and doing a two-pass sort gives deterministic, correct ordering without faking distinct dates.

### Why this is non-obvious
Liquid has no multi-key sort syntax — there's no `sort: 'date', 'order'` option. The standard workaround people reach for is either: (a) faking distinct dates, or (b) building complex where/concat pipelines to manually group and sequence items. The stable sort property is not documented in the Jekyll or Liquid docs — it's an implementation detail of Ruby's `Array#sort_by`, which is the underlying mechanism.

### Caveat
Items without the secondary key (e.g. `order: nil`) sort inconsistently across Liquid versions — place them in a separate array and concat if you need predictable nil handling.
