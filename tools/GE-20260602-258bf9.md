## What happens

Technical documentation serves two audiences: humans who scan headings and labels to extract facts quickly, and LLMs that load the document as session context and need unambiguous, precisely stated facts. The instinct is to trade one against the other — more structure for LLMs means less readable prose for humans.

## The technique

**The label is the fact. The body is the reasoning.**

Every entry — wiring point, key file, gotcha — has a label (bold lead-in, heading, or structured prefix) that contains a complete, standalone fact. The body adds reasoning, context, or explanation. Neither audience loses anything.

**Strip test:** Remove all labels and read only those in sequence. The result should be a complete factual skeleton with no gaps. If a fact exists only in prose body text, it is invisible to scanning and invisible to LLMs parsing structure.

**Example (before — fact buried in body):**
> The class loader picks up resources from the JAR at test time, which is why edits don't apply without a rebuild.

**Example (after — fact in label, reasoning in body):**
> **Resource files load from JAR at test time — no edit-and-refresh.** `pr-review.yaml` lives in `review/src/main/resources/`. The Quarkus classloader picks it up from the compiled JAR, not the source directory. Changes require a rebuild.

## Why non-obvious

The assumption is that serving LLMs means more verbosity (repeated context, explicit labels) while serving humans means more brevity (assume shared context, no repetition). The non-obvious resolution: labels serve both simultaneously. A specific label is scannable for humans AND an unambiguous anchor for LLMs. Specificity and scannability reinforce rather than conflict.
