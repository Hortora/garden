## What it is

When scoring candidates (use cases, technologies, approaches) across multiple dimensions, split the dimensions into two or more purpose-specific tables rather than producing a single combined score. Each table answers a different question. The split reveals the *shape* of each candidate's strengths — something a combined score averages away.

## When to use it

Any evaluation where candidates are being assessed for multiple distinct purposes simultaneously. The signal is: you find yourself explaining "this scores well overall but is weak on X" — that explanation means a combined score is hiding useful information.

## The pattern

**Example: evaluating tutorial use cases for a developer platform**

Instead of one table with 8 dimensions and a single /40 score:

```
Table 1: Market Fit (5 dimensions — can we justify entering this market?)
  Platform differentiation | Compliance necessity | Market entry gap | Comparison richness | Market size

Table 2: Community Fit (5 dimensions — does this work as a developer tutorial?)
  Java/Quarkus relatability | Human relatability | Tutorial teachability | LLM depth | Accountability drama
```

**What the split revealed that combined scores hid:**

Drug discovery and clinical trials both scored 24/25 on Market Fit — identical. But drug discovery scored 15/25 on Community Fit vs clinical trials' 19/25. A single combined score (39 vs 43) shows clinical trials wins, but obscures *why*: drug discovery is an equally strong market entry argument but completely unusable as a developer tutorial.

Security incident response scored 22/25 on Community Fit — strong — but only 19/25 on Market Fit because the SOAR market is dominated by large incumbents. The combined score (41) looks similar to clinical trials (43) but the shape is completely different: one is a market play, the other is a tutorial vehicle.

## Why non-obvious

Single combined scores are the default because they produce a simple rank order. The instinct is to add more dimensions for precision, which makes the combined score noisier without revealing shape. The counter-intuitive move is to *separate* dimensions by purpose and accept multiple partial rankings — then look at which candidates score consistently vs which are strong on one axis and weak on the other.

**How to apply:** Define the tables before scoring, not after. Ask "what distinct questions are we trying to answer?" — each answer maps to a table. If you find yourself adding a tiebreaker dimension, it probably belongs in a separate table.
