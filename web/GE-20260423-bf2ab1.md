## Symptom

Terrain tiles assigned a warm sandy-brown `MeshLambertMaterial` colour (e.g. `0xb8956a`) still render as grey or cool blue-grey in the scene. Changing the material colour alone has no visible effect on the perceived hue.

## Context

Three.js `MeshLambertMaterial` computes final colour as `material_color × light_color`. When the primary `DirectionalLight` is blue-white (`0xaabbff`), the blue channel dominates and overwhelms any warm tones in the material. The effect is most pronounced on mid-range desaturated colours — a sandy brown renders as indistinguishable grey.

## What was tried

Changing the `mGround` material colour from dark blue-grey (`0x1a2233`) to warm sandy brown (`0xb8956a`). The tiles still appeared grey. Increasing the material brightness did not help — the hue stayed cool.

## Root cause

`MeshLambertMaterial` performs per-vertex diffuse shading: `result = material × Σ(light_color × intensity × NdotL)`. With sun `0xaabbff` at intensity 1.3 and ambient `0x223355` at 0.9, the blue channel contribution is high enough to shift any warm colour toward grey. The material colour is effectively tinted by the light before rendering.

## Fix

Change the `DirectionalLight` colour to neutral white and the ambient to a neutral or warm tone before adjusting material colours:

```js
// Before (blue-white — kills warm tones):
scene.add(new THREE.AmbientLight(0x223355, 0.9));
const sun = new THREE.DirectionalLight(0xaabbff, 1.3);

// After (neutral — lets material colour read through):
scene.add(new THREE.AmbientLight(0x706050, 0.8));
const sun = new THREE.DirectionalLight(0xffffff, 1.2);
```

Then set the material colour to the desired hue — it will appear close to the raw hex value.

## Why this is non-obvious

The instinct is to change the material colour; the lighting is rarely the first thing inspected. The blue tint is subtle enough that it reads as "the colour isn't warm enough" rather than "the light is fighting me". The fix requires changing the sun, not the material.
